**Series: Beware the Beginnings – Reclaiming Our Local Narrative**

A healthy democracy is loud, transparent, and occasionally inconvenient for those in power. It requires citizens who can question authority and a government that operates in the open. But lately, in Australia, the lights are being turned out one by one.

### The Shrinking Space for Dissent

In recent years, we have seen a concerning reduction in protest rights across the country. Laws designed to protect "critical infrastructure" are increasingly used to criminalize peaceful activists and community groups. What begins as a move against "extremists" often expands to capture anyone who objects to government policy.

For our community in Boothby, this matters. Whether it's residents passionate about protecting our coastline or those concerned about the future of our local schools, everyone should be able to exercise their democratic right to dissent without fear of disproportionate criminal penalties. 

### The War on Information

Alongside the erosion of protest rights is a sustained attack on transparency. The Freedom of Information (FOI) Act, once a tool for accountability, has become a bureaucratic obstacle course. Requests are routinely delayed or denied on grounds that stretch the definition of "public interest" beyond recognition.

This culture of secrecy serves the powerful. It allows multinational corporations to negotiate with government behind closed doors and ensures that the influence of major political donors remains hidden from public view. When the National Anti-Corruption Commission (NACC) was established, many hoped for a new era of integrity, but independent MP Helen Haines has documented that it still lacks the teeth needed to truly hold the powerful to account.

### The Path to Accountability

Independent representatives have shown that we don't have to accept this slide toward secrecy. By championing integrity measures and demanding stronger whistleblower protections, they prove that representation can be a tool for transparency rather than a shield for the status quo.

An independent voice for Boothby would:
*   **Support genuine FOI reform** to make government documents public by default.
*   **Oppose surveillance expansions** that lack meaningful oversight and transparency.
*   **Advocate for a stronger NACC** with the power to investigate and publicise improper conduct.

Our best defense against a "quieter democracy" is to stay informed and stay connected. By building a movement that is transparent and accountable to each other, we create a network that cannot be easily silenced.
