**Series: Who Really Decides? – Reclaiming Our Local Narrative**

In an electorate like Boothby, we pride ourselves on being a community for everyone—from retirees in Belair who have lived here for forty years to young families trying to buy their first home in Seacliff or Aberfoyle Park. We value the generational continuity that makes our suburbs feel like home.

But our federal tax system is actively working against that balance. Specifically, the **Capital Gains Tax (CGT) discount** has become a significant barrier for the next generation.

### The Mathematics of Inequality

Australia's housing market has undergone a profound transformation. In 1999, when the CGT discount was introduced, the national median house price was approximately $220,000. Today, in sought-after suburbs like those in Boothby, prices have ballooned, often exceeding $1 million.

This is not simply natural market growth. It is the consequence of a tax system that treats housing as a speculative investment. The 50% CGT discount means that when a property investor sells an asset, only half of that gain is subject to tax. When combined with negative gearing, it creates a powerful incentive for investors to outbid those looking for a place to live.

The impact is stark. Research from the Parliamentary Budget Office shows these concessions overwhelmingly benefit the top 10% of income earners. Meanwhile, the Australian Council of Trade Unions has documented that a young person today needs to save for approximately **13 years** for a 20% deposit—compared to just 5 years in the 1980s.

### A System That Penalises the Young

There is something deeply unjust about a tax system that asks young Australians to work harder and save longer than any previous generation, while simultaneously subsidising those who already own multiple properties. 

In Boothby, we see this play out every day. A nurse at Flinders Medical Centre, a teacher at a local primary school, or a small business owner—each of them faces a reality where house prices have far outstripped wage growth. When we subsidise investment losses with taxpayer money, we are effectively asking the next generation to fund the very mechanism that is pricing them out of their own community.

The estimated $10 billion annual cost of these concessions could instead be used to:
*   Fund significant investment in social and affordable housing.
*   Reduce university fees for the next generation of professionals.
*   Expand Medicare to include essential dental care.

### The Need for a Brave Conversation

Both major parties have historically treated the CGT discount as an untouchable political landmine. This leads to a stalemate where the status quo remains untouched while the housing crisis deepens.

An independent representative, answerable only to the people of Boothby, can facilitate a more honest conversation. This isn't about "attacking" success; it's about ensuring the tax code isn't rigged against our children and grandchildren. We can transition toward a fairer system—perhaps through phased changes—while ensuring that the primary purpose of housing is to provide a home. 

Every year we delay reform, the dream of home ownership slips further away for young families in Brighton, Glenelg, and Blackwood. It’s time for a representative who has the courage to think beyond the next election cycle and advocate for a "fair go" that actually includes everyone.
