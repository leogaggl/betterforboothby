**Series: Who Really Decides? – Reclaiming Our Local Narrative**

It is one of the most striking paradoxes in the Australian economy: we export roughly the same amount of gas as Qatar, yet Qatar collects nearly **twenty times** more in royalties and taxes. While we are often told there is "no money" for expanding dental care under Medicare or properly funding our local schools, we are overseeing a system that effectively gives our national resources away to multinational corporations.

### The Japanese Comparison: A National Irony

A recent Senate inquiry revealed a staggering reality: **The Japanese Government generates more revenue taxing Australian gas imports than the Australian Government makes from exporting that same gas.**

As Dr Richard Denniss from the Australia Institute has pointed out, we are essentially subsidising foreign budgets with our own resources. While Australian households face rising energy bills, the wealth generated from our offshore gas—worth approximately $100 billion in exports last year—yields an effective tax rate of barely 1.5% through the Petroleum Resource Rent Tax (PRRT).

### From the Coast to the Hills: What We Lose

When we talk about "resource rents" and tax policy, it can feel distant from daily life in Boothby. But the connection is direct. The $14.5 billion in annual subsidies currently provided to the fossil fuel industry is money that isn't available for the services our community relies on.

Imagine what a fairer share of our resource wealth could do right here:
*   **Healthcare:** We are told Medicare is "unsustainable," yet ending these subsidies could fund a significant expansion of bulk-billing services and reduce wait times at Flinders Medical Centre.
*   **Education:** We could properly fund our public schools and relieve the pressure on local families struggling with the rising costs of university and vocational training.
*   **Climate Resilience:** From protecting our coastline between Brighton and Seacliff to managing bushfire risks in the Blackwood hills, a fairer tax system would provide the resources needed for genuine, local climate adaptation.

### Choosing a Voice for a Fairer Go

This isn't about being "anti-business"—it’s about a fair go for our community. The reason these policies persist is that the fossil fuel lobby has unparalleled access in Canberra, often drowning out the voices of everyday Australians. 

When our representatives are bound to a national party machine, they find it difficult to challenge the donors who fund their campaigns. A community-backed independent doesn't have to answer to a party boss or a corporate lobbyist. They are free to ask the simple question: *Why aren't the people of Boothby seeing the benefit of our own resources?*

By choosing representation that puts our community first, we can start a conversation about how Australia’s wealth can be used to build a better future for everyone.
